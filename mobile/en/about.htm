Who I am

Just another noise maker, the story of whom I will be trying to sum up as shortly as I can.


My fate became definitively sealed on this summer day of year 2003, when I saw through the window of destiny, in the dusty darkness of an old and tired attic, what long ago had been a guitar, and then was desperately begging both Heaven and Hell, with its poor wounded arms, asking for me to give it a decent dying. Even if our meeting was most likely nothing more than a happy coincidence, we both welcomed it as the sign of predilection, and therefore embraced each other with candid and impatient fury, as if we had been young lovers.


The reason why I felt this way is that I had earlier been seduced by the Sex Pistols' “idontgiveashitness” and by M. Hendrix' easy-looking virtuosity — they sit among the first of my teachers' infinite list.
From this day on, I spent most of my time between rehearsal rooms and squatted buildings, in both of which dust and beer subtly merged in the air. At that time my collection of instruments grew as much as my tastes became less sectarian: Punk music leading to Garage rock, which leads to either Surf music or Acid rock, the latter leading to either Krautrock, Protest song or Blues, et cætera, et cætera, et cætera...


And there was evening and there was morning. Then I had to stop hanging around in celibacy's recklessness and misery to start looking for material comfort: time for me to get a job. So I sold my strength to the restless consumption of this race called human kind who is too indolent to ensure its own long term survival ; and thus I lacked the daily hours of practice a musician needs.
But, once more, Fate spoke. And since I was simply unable to let music go, I remembered that Kubrick once gave me an underlying passion for Handel, Purcell, Schubert and, above all, for Ludwig van. This had me decide to leave the oral tradition behind and deepen my poor knowledge in written music by studying scores and treatises (Counterpoint, Harmony and, more recently, Orchestration) all along Paris' suburban railways.


It soon gave me ample enough to repopulate my teachers' pantheon, so that the humble Robert Johnson or the decadent Velvet Underground now get along with a completely different kind of immortals, the first of whom happened to be Fux, infallibly followed by the priceless Bach, then Telemann, then Pachelbel, then Corelli, Scarlatti, Tartini, Raison, Marais, Couperin, Rameau, Haydn, Mozart, Clementi, Reicha, Field, Paganini, Weber, Mendelssohn, Schumann, Chopin, Berlioz, Liszt, Wagner, Brahms, Smetana, Dvořák, Balakirev, Mussorgsky, Saint-Saëns, Fauré, Koechlin, Debussy, Scriabin, Strauss, Wolf, Mahler, Zemlinsky, Schoenberg, Berg, Webern, Stravinsky, Szymanowski, Bartók, Britten, Prokofiev, Křenek, Schulhoff, Messiaen, Wyschnegradsky, Lutosławski, Ligeti, Babbit, Henze, Boulez, Johnston, Penderecki, Aho...


(To you, whose name is Legion, and who I ineluctably forgot, my most sincere and respectful apologies.)


Eventhough I am far from claiming that such renowned references allow me any comparison, I get tireless self-dissatisfaction and the constant self-improvement thus implied out of it. Yet I do not disown nor denigrate any of the popular music that first seduced me — let it be my unique concession to the hate I show toward Arts' labeling: I do approve postmodern eclecticism.


Unfortunate digression: I was almost done!


Along with skills gradually came self-confidence, and the latter gave birth to hope ; the one of a carrer revolution as the life-saving catharsis for an artist whose frustrated prolixity inspired himself poems which started to make writers such as Messieurs de Nerval, Arthaud or John of Patmos sound like jolly, cheerful and healthy-minded writers. The solution came from fortuitous recollection of youth (and even childhood) idols like Maurice Jarre, Ennio Morricone, Wendy Carlos, John Williams or Bernard Herrmann ; indeed, at the highest stage of the society of the spectacle, music is a profession as long as it tends to remain part of the global show, for example by underscoring a motion picture.


Having had this eye-opener, I immediately left my cash drawer and put myself to the trouble of being born: I learnt the secrets of film scoring by training on silent films by Eisenstein, and offered Papa the fruits of my labour, hoping that he would find them a useful place in the documentaries he makes.
Which eventually happened and allowed me to increase my CV with the Experience chapter, so that today, finally, I feel legitimate enough to publish this website.


Stop talking, let's go write what happens next!
